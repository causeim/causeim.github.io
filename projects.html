Toward Most Agile and Cost Effective Life-sized Robot

A Coaxial Spherical Parallel Mechanism that centralizes four actuators at the pelvis and distributes load equally across them — quadrupling effective strength and extending gear life 64× without scaling up the motors.

Overview

Life-sized legged robots are held back by a hard trade-off: bigger actuators make the limb heavier, which demands even bigger actuators. The Coaxial Spherical Parallel Mechanism breaks that cycle by centralizing all four actuators at the pelvis and arranging their output axes coaxially so that every actuator contributes to every direction of motion. Vertical, radial, and lateral load on the end effector is shared equally — turning the "4 actuators per leg" count into real capability rather than mass.

Why it matters. When each gear sees only ¼ of the load, a Wöhler-style fatigue model predicts gear life scales as (1/4)-3 = 64×. The same mechanism also lets motor and gear sizes shrink — the limb gets lighter while getting stronger.

Design Principles

  • Coaxial layout — all four actuator outputs are aligned on a common axis, so every actuator's torque projects onto the vertical direction during stance.
  • Equal load distribution — vertical, radial, and lateral loads on the end effector are divided across the four actuators rather than carried by one.
  • Centralized mass at the pelvis — lower limb inertia makes swing faster and modeling errors smaller for whole-body control.
  • Thermal management — all heat-generating elements live in one location, simplifying cooling and cable routing.
  • Analytic kinematics — despite being a parallel mechanism, the closed-form solution is as simple as a serial link, keeping control cheap.

Gear-life Scaling

The load-sharing effect compounds through the gear's fatigue curve. A common Wöhler-style S-N model for rolling-contact fatigue goes as N ∝ σ-3. Reducing the per-gear stress to ¼ therefore multiplies the predicted cycles-to-failure by (¼)-3 = 64. The same mechanism that quadruples effective strength also buys roughly two orders of magnitude of service life without a beefier actuator.

Performance Envelope

Force capacity mapped across the leg's vertical workspace, split by limiting regime. The motor-limited map bounds instantaneous capability at high joint speeds; the gear-limited map bounds the sustainable, repeatable peak used for sizing.

Motion & Control

Parallel pose sequences and closed-loop tracking from the simulator/rig. Because limb mass is centralized at the pelvis, the dynamic model stays well-conditioned even at high swing rates — tracking RMS stays tight across the six-second test window.

Applications

1. Bipedal Robot

Each leg is a 4-DOF Coaxial SPM. Because mass is centralized near the body, whole-body dynamics have lower modeling error and control can run at short computation horizons despite the parallel structure. Continuous Ground Reaction Force in some regions reaches twice the projected body weight — giving useful margin for jumping and perturbation recovery.

4 DOF / leg GRF > 2× body weight ~35 kg biped target

2. Robot Arm & Manipulator

Using the same mechanism as a manipulator, payload is amplified 4× versus a single-actuator baseline while moving-part mass is reduced. Capacity exceeds 40 kg across the full workspace. The design favors serviceability: only 14 bolts to disassemble the arm, and 6 to remove an actuator set.

> 40 kg payload 4× payload amplification 14-bolt teardown
